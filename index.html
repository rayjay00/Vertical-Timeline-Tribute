George Washington

∙A Timeline of Life∙

1732

George Washington was born to Augustine and Mary Washington in Westmoreland County, Virginia. His grandfather, John Washington, had emigrated to Virginia in 1656 to farm. George's father, Augustine, farmed tobacco and Justice of the Westmoreland County Court. Augustine's death would prevent George from going to school in England and his mother refused to grant him permission to join the Royal Navy.

1754

The colonies of Virginia and Pennsylvania grew suspicious when the French began expanding into the Ohio Country in 1753. This expansion eventually led to the conflict known as the French and Indian War and to the start of the Seven Years' War. Washington delivered a letter to the French asking them to vacate the Ohio Valley but the French refused to comply. Washington now officially began his military career by getting a commission to raise a company of 100 men. In May 1754, Washington and his small militia ambushed the French and won a small battle. The French responded by attacking and capturing Washington at Fort Necessity but released him and his troops to return to Virginia.

1775

It was in this year that Washington formally took command of the Continental Army. Washington gained experience as a soldier in the French and Indian War and had been appointed commander in chief by the Continental Congress. Washington led the Continental Army without accepting any money.

1781

On October 19, 1781, Great Britain surrendered at Yorktown, Virginia. British General Charles Cornwallis led about 8,000 troops to Yorktown expecting naval reinforcements. The reinforcements never arrived and the formal surrender of the British occurred when General O'Hara carried Cornwallis' sword to the American and French commanders. It wouldn't be until 1783, when the Treaty of Paris was signed, that the United States of America was formally recognized.

1785

The Mount Vernon Conference was one of the earliest formal meetings of delegates. It was a precursor to later Constitutional Conventions. During the Mount Vernon Conferences, delegates discussed navigational rights in the states' common waterways and the regulation of commerce between states.

1789

Washington was inaugurated as the first President of the United States on April 30, 1789. The ceremony was held on the balcony of the Federal Hall in New York City. Washington became the first member of the executive branch to carry out the Constitution of 1787.

1793

Washington issued the Proclamation of Neutrality that declared the United States neutral in the conflict between France and Great Britain. It threatened legal proceedings against any American providing assistance to any country at war.

1794

The Whiskey Rebellion was a tax potest during Washington's presidency. It was a response to the first tax imposed on a domestic product. Citizens throughout Western Pennsylvania used violence to prevent federal officials from collecting the tax. Washington himself ended up riding to the area to suppress the insurgency along with 13,000 militiamen.

1797

After eight years as President, Washington retired in March 1797 at the age of 65. Washington dedicated much of his time to his farm and to his distillery. The next year, Washington was offered a commission as lieutenant general on July 4, 1798. He accepted and served until his death seventeen months later.

1799

Washington's death cam quite suddenly. After tending to his farm on horseback one evening he began to feel very ill with a sore throat. His condition became progressively worse and doctors unsuccessfully used bloodletting to treat his condition. One doctor proposed using a tracheotomy to help stabilize Washington's labored breathing, but the others disapproved. Washington dies at around 10 p.m. on December 17, 1799, at the age of 67. A funeral was held at Mount Vernon and memorial processions occurred around the country.
